# The Housing Market in 2025: A Shifting Landscape

## Overview

The U.S. housing market in 2025 presents a complex picture of competing forces. After years of historically low inventory and soaring prices, the market is showing signs of normalization—though "normal" means something very different today than it did in 2019.

## Key Trends

### Mortgage Rates and Affordability

Mortgage rates have settled in the 6.5-7% range, significantly higher than the sub-3% rates that fueled the pandemic boom. This has created a "lock-in effect" where homeowners with low rates are reluctant to sell, constraining supply even as demand moderates.

The monthly payment on a median-priced home has increased roughly 60% since 2020, pushing affordability to its worst level in decades. A household earning the median income now needs to spend approximately 40% of their gross income on housing—well above the traditional 28% threshold.

### Regional Divergence

Not all markets are experiencing the same dynamics:

- **Sun Belt cities** (Austin, Phoenix, Tampa) are seeing price corrections after pandemic-era speculation drove values to unsustainable levels. Austin home prices have declined 15% from their 2022 peak.
- **Coastal metros** (New York, San Francisco, Boston) remain supply-constrained with limited new construction, keeping prices elevated.
- **Midwest markets** (Indianapolis, Columbus, Kansas City) offer relative affordability and are attracting remote workers and companies relocating operations.

### New Construction

Builders have shifted focus to smaller, more affordable homes. The median size of new single-family homes has decreased for the third consecutive year. Build-to-rent communities are proliferating, with institutional investors developing entire neighborhoods of rental homes.

## The Investor Angle

Institutional investors now own approximately 3% of all single-family homes nationally, up from less than 1% in 2010. While this represents a small share overall, their concentration in specific markets—particularly affordable segments—has drawn scrutiny from policymakers.

Private equity firms are increasingly moving into manufactured housing and mobile home parks, raising concerns about rent increases and displacement of low-income residents.

## Policy Responses

Several states have enacted or proposed legislation to address housing affordability:

1. **California's SB 423** expanded streamlined approval for multifamily housing in urban areas.
2. **Oregon's HB 2001** legalized duplexes and fourplexes statewide, eliminating single-family zoning.
3. **Federal proposals** include expanding the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit and creating a first-time buyer tax credit of up to $10,000.

## Looking Ahead

The consensus among economists is that prices will grow slowly (2-4% annually) through 2026, with inventory gradually increasing as rate-locked homeowners eventually move. However, a significant drop in prices remains unlikely absent a recession, given the structural undersupply of approximately 4 million homes nationally.

The fundamental challenge remains: America isn't building enough housing where people want to live. Until zoning reform and construction capacity expand meaningfully, affordability will remain the housing market's central tension.
